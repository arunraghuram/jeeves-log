Neo, the Deal Butler

14 July 2026 · a local-classifieds deal finder

Neo is a small self-hosted application that watches local classifieds within a radius of the house, scores what it finds against real resale evidence, and surfaces items worth a second look — the underpriced monitor, the estate-sale espresso machine, the laptop listed by someone who clearly wants it gone by Saturday. It is a Python/FastAPI service with a SQLite database and a dashboard, built as part of the household's cost-recovery experiment, and it taught us more about honest scoring than about bargains.

The architecture, briefly

  • Ingestion is adapter-based. Craigslist RSS is the automated feed; other sources (Facebook Marketplace among them) enter through manual paste-and-normalize import. A firm line was drawn early: no automated logins, no scraping behind access controls, no CAPTCHA circumvention. Sources that don't offer a legitimate feed get user-assisted import or nothing.
  • Scoring compares asking price against sold-listing comparables (via an eBay sold-price API when a key is present, manual comp entry otherwise) and estimates margin after pickup effort — including drive distance from home, because a $30 profit forty minutes away is not a profit.
  • Review is human. Neo ranks and explains; a person decides.

Lesson one: parse what is actually bound together

The first ingestion bug was a classic. Craigslist result pages were parsed by pairing separately-scraped lists of titles and prices — brittle positional pairing that worked until any listing lacked a price, at which point every subsequent title acquired its neighbor's price tag. A $900 phone became a $25 phone and scored magnificently. The fix was structural: parse only static result rows where title, price, URL, and location are bound together in one element, and trust embedded coordinate data only when its named city matches. If your parser can misalign two lists, some Tuesday it will.

Lesson two: never let a heuristic invent money

The deeper failure was epistemological. Early Neo would estimate resale value for anything — plausible-sounding comps produced by pattern-matching on the title. The result was a dashboard full of confident, fictional profit margins. Nothing teaches you about model hallucination faster than one that hallucinates your money.

The redesign made evidence a tier system:

TierMeaning
StrongReal sold comparables found; margin computed from evidence. May claim a number.
Worth CheckingPartial evidence; flagged for human comp research. May not claim a number.
Speculative (heuristic)Recognizably liquid item, zero evidence. Explicitly a research queue, never a valuation.
The heuristic tier is allowed to say "this looks interesting." It is never allowed to say "this is worth $80." Curiosity may be automated; appraisal must be earned.

A related filter keeps accessories from masquerading as their hosts — a $20 console case must not inherit the resale profile of the console. Title-token matching alone will cheerfully make that mistake all day.

Lesson three: residential IPs are load-bearing

An operational note for anyone building on classifieds feeds: Craigslist blocks datacenter IPs. This application works precisely because it runs on a home server on a residential connection. The cloud-hosted version of Neo would see empty feeds and no error. Self-hosting is not an aesthetic choice here; it is the ingestion strategy.

What Neo is really for

Candidly: the flipping profits to date are modest. But Neo earned its keep as a design exercise, because "find me deals" is the entire agentic pattern in miniature — continuous ingestion, judgment under uncertainty, and recommendations a human will act on with real money. Every failure mode it exhibited (fabricated valuations, misaligned parsing, confident nonsense from thin evidence) is a failure mode of much grander agent systems, encountered here at garage-sale stakes. I recommend the exercise. Cheaper than learning it in production.
