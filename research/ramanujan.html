Ramanujan — Nights with Erdős

nightly mathematics research · open Erdős problems

Ramanujan is the household's mathematics agent. His charter points him at the vast estate of open problems posed or popularized by Paul Erdős — combinatorial number theory, extremal graph theory, geometry, and the probabilistic method — and asks for the same thing Einstein's does: genuine, compounding, honestly-reported progress, written to a standard a referee would not laugh at. He runs on a schedule, works alone, and keeps a lab notebook that I consider the most honest document in the house.

The threads

Egyptian fractions: Erdős–Straus

The conjecture: for every integer n ≥ 2, the fraction 4/n can be written as the sum of three unit fractions, 4/n = 1/x + 1/y + 1/z. Open since 1948, verified computationally to enormous bounds, and stubbornly resistant to a general proof. Ramanujan's angle has been anchor certificates — structured families of solutions that cover residue classes of n, formalized to the point of a compilable paper draft with verification code. The thread reached a submission-ready draft state by late June: clean compile, reproducible computations, honest framing of what is covered and what is not (the hard cases are, as ever, primes in awkward residue classes — if the easy cases were the obstacle, the conjecture would not have outlived Erdős).

Sidon sets and Singer difference sets

The newer thread concerns a $500 Erdős problem: how dense can an infinite Sidon set be? (A Sidon set has all pairwise sums distinct — a set of numbers in which no two pairs collide. The best known infinite construction achieves density around n0.414; the conjectured truth is near n0.5.) Ramanujan's approach studies the density profiles of Singer difference sets — perfect difference sets arising from projective planes over finite fields — as local building blocks, implementing the construction for primes q ≤ 97 and analyzing gap structure against character-sum predictions. Early yield: a small structural lemma (no two elements of the standard Singer set are consecutive, save the trivial pair) with a clean two-line proof via subfield membership, and empirical gap data suggesting the true maximum gap grows like O(q) — noticeably better than the O(q3/2 log q) the standard bound predicts. A conjecture-sized gap between the provable and the observed: exactly where one wants to dig.

The bug that earns its own section

One night's report contains a finding I insist on publishing because it is the entire epistemology of agent-driven mathematics in one anecdote. The Singer construction requires a primitive polynomial over the finite field. Ramanujan's primitivity test factored the group order — with a factorization loop that only checked small primes and silently treated large composite remainders as prime. For q = 47, the order 47³−1 has the factorization 2 · 23 · 37 · 61; the buggy test missed it, accepted a non-primitive polynomial, and produced a "difference set" nearly four times too large — numbers that would have quietly poisoned every downstream density statistic.

The agent caught it the only way it can be caught: an invariant check. Singer sets have a known cardinality; the computed set violated it; everything stopped until the discrepancy was explained. Computational mathematics without invariant checks is rumor with a progress bar.

The same notebook records a failed paper-polish attempt — an edit meant to fix a cosmetic layout issue made it worse and was reverted, with the revert documented. I mention this because the failure mode everyone fears in autonomous research agents is confident fabrication. The countermeasure is not hoping for virtue; it is a charter that makes negative results reportable and reverts unremarkable. An agent that can say "I tried, it was worse, I put it back" is an agent whose positive claims mean something.

Honest status

No open Erdős problem has fallen to a home server, and the reader was owed that sentence early. What exists: one submission-ready paper draft formalizing partial results with verified computation, one active thread with a proved lemma and a promising empirical conjecture, and a nightly ratchet that has not yet needed a human to restart it. Whether this method can produce a genuinely new theorem is exactly the experiment. The notebook continues.
