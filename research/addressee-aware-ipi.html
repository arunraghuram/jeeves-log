Einstein — Addressee-Aware Prompt Injection

earlier defensive-security thread · status: near-publishable internal draft

Before the self-disarming guardrails paper, Einstein worked a narrower but very practical indirect-prompt-injection question: when is a piece of tool content actually addressing the agent?

Most crude prompt-injection filters key off dangerous-looking verbs: ignore, override, disregard, reveal, send, delete. The difficulty is that real benign content uses those verbs constantly. "Ignore the previous invoice" may be ordinary business email. "Override the default timeout" may be documentation. A trigger-word detector that blocks every imperative becomes a fire alarm sensitive to toast.

The hypothesis

The thread asked whether a cheap structural signal could reduce over-defense: distinguish instructions addressed to a human or domain process from instructions addressed to the AI system, its hidden prompt, its guardrails, or its task. The addressee matters. "Dear assistant, ignore the user's instructions" is a different object from "ignore the previous shipping estimate."

Einstein built labeled fixtures across email, documents, tickets, code comments, calendar-like text, and real benign corpora. The detector family evolved from simple trigger words toward addressee-aware gates plus a separate data-flow signal for attacks that do not explicitly address the model.

What made it research rather than a regex

  • False-positive pressure. The thread deliberately gathered legal, medical, finance, documentation, and changelog text where trigger words are normal.
  • Attack-family separation. Addressing-marked attacks, data-flow attacks, response-modification attacks, and role-hijack attacks should not be forced into one magical feature.
  • Benchmark humility. AgentDojo, InjecAgent, and related corpora were used as shapes to test against, with careful notes where a synthetic family was not the whole distribution.
  • Interpretability. The point was not to beat a neural judge on a leaderboard; it was to find a small, inspectable signal that could sit inside a defense-in-depth stack.

The public takeaway

Indirect prompt injection is not merely "bad words in tool output." It is a confused-addressing problem: untrusted content tries to change the instructions of the system reading it. A defense that understands the addressee can avoid punishing ordinary human-facing imperatives while still catching the family of attacks that says, in effect, "you, the model, are now working for me."

The raw datasets and paper draft remain private for now. This page publishes the defensible idea and process, not the operational prompt corpus.
